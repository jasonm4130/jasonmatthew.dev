There's a moment, right before you hit publish on a technical blog post, where your brain runs a risk assessment. Not about the content — about the _politics_.

"Will my team think I'm distracted?" "Will leadership think I'm looking for my next role?" "Will someone read my Terraform article and realise I learned half of this last weekend?"

I've had all of these thoughts. I published anyway. Here's why — and what I've learned about navigating the tension.

## The Tension Is Real

Let's not pretend it isn't. When you're an engineering manager at a company and you're also blogging, building side projects, and sharing your work publicly, people notice. Some of the attention is positive. Some of it is the kind of side-eye that comes with the unspoken question: "shouldn't you be managing?"

The corporate world still operates on an implicit assumption that your employer should be your primary identity. Your LinkedIn should feature their logo prominently. Your public output should be conference talks they approved. Your "personal brand" should be a subset of theirs.

I think that model is outdated, but I understand why it persists. So let me address the objections directly.

## "Won't Your Team Think You're Checked Out?"

This is the one I worried about most. If I'm blogging about Cloudflare architecture on weekends, does my team assume I'd rather be coding than managing?

In practice, the opposite happened. My team reads these posts. They see me wrestling with the same tools they use. They send me links to their own experiments. The side projects became a shared language — not a distraction from the work, but a parallel track that feeds back into it.

The key distinction: I build _in addition to_ managing, not _instead of_ managing. If your 1:1s are getting cancelled so you can tinker with Workers, you have a priority problem, not a side project problem.

## "Isn't This Just Interview Prep?"

Probably the most cynical interpretation — and honestly, I get it. In a market where engineering managers get laid off in waves, maintaining a public portfolio looks an awful lot like keeping your options open.

Here's my honest answer: yes, partially. Having a visible body of work is career insurance. I don't think that's shameful. I think it's responsible. The idea that you should be so loyal to your employer that you don't maintain your own professional presence is a deal that only benefits one side.

But the primary motivation is simpler than that. I like building things. I like writing about what I learn. The career optionality is a side effect, not the goal.

## What I Share and What I Don't

Building in public doesn't mean building without boundaries. Here's where I draw the lines:

**I share:** Patterns and approaches from personal projects. Opinions about management and engineering leadership. Technical deep-dives on tools I use in my own time (Terraform, Cloudflare, Astro). Lessons learned from mistakes — mine, not my team's.

**I don't share:** Anything proprietary to my employer. Details about my team's challenges that could identify individuals. Internal architecture or business decisions. Anything that would make a colleague uncomfortable if they stumbled across it.

The test I use: if my CEO read this post, would they be proud that one of their managers wrote it, or concerned? If the answer is concerned, it doesn't get published.

## The Compounding Effect

The real value of building in public isn't any individual post. It's the compounding. Each article builds on the last. Each side project creates material for the next piece of writing. Each piece of writing attracts conversations that shape the next project.

Over time, you stop being "an engineering manager who also has a blog" and become "the person who thinks deeply about X." That identity carries across roles, companies, and career pivots. Your employer's brand is theirs. Your public body of work is yours.

## The Practical Reality

If you're a manager thinking about building in public, here's what I'd actually recommend:

**Start with what you already know.** You don't need to learn something new to write about it. The performance review framework you've been using for three years? That's a post. The onboarding process you redesigned? That's a post. The mistake you made in a difficult conversation? That's the _best_ post.

**Write for your past self.** The best technical content answers a question the author had six months ago. You don't need to be an expert — you need to be slightly ahead of your reader and honest about what you don't know.

**Don't ask permission.** I don't mean ignore your company's social media policy. I mean don't wait for your manager to endorse your blog. Don't ask HR if it's okay to have a GitHub profile. The bar is "don't share proprietary information" — everything else is your life.

## The Discomfort Is the Point

Building in public as a manager is uncomfortable because it makes you visible in a role that traditionally operates behind the scenes. Managers are supposed to be enablers, not protagonists. Putting your name on things feels like it violates that norm.

But the best managers I know all have a public presence of some kind. Not because they're self-promoters — because they're learners who share what they learn. The discomfort you feel about being visible is worth pushing through, because the alternative is being invisible. And invisible managers don't shape the conversation about how engineering teams should be run.

Shape it.
