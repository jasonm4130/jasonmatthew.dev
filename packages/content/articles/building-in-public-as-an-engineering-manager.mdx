There's a moment, right before you hit publish on a technical blog post, where your brain runs a risk assessment. Not about the content — about the _politics_.

"Will my team think I'm distracted?" "Will leadership think I'm looking for my next role?" "Will someone read my Terraform article and realise I learned half of this last weekend?"

I've had all of these thoughts. I published anyway. Here's why — and what I've learned about navigating the tension.

## The Tension Is Real

Let's not pretend it isn't. When you're an engineering manager at a company and you're also blogging, building side projects, and sharing your work publicly, people notice. Some of the attention is positive. Some of it is the kind of side-eye that comes with the unspoken question: "shouldn't you be managing?"

The corporate world still operates on an implicit assumption that your employer should be your primary identity. Your LinkedIn should feature their logo prominently. Your public output should be conference talks they approved. Your "personal brand" should be a subset of theirs.

I think that model is outdated, but I understand why it persists. So let me address the objections directly.

## "Won't Your Team Think You're Checked Out?"

This is the one I worried about most. If I'm blogging about Cloudflare architecture on weekends, does my team assume I'd rather be coding than managing?

In practice, the opposite happened. I share what I'm building with my team and they find it interesting. More importantly, they share their side projects with me — and I think they're really cool. It goes both ways. Side projects become a shared language, not a distraction from the work.

It's kind of like Google's 20% time, except for people who don't work at Google or FAANG companies. Engineers learning new tools, experimenting with ideas, solving their own problems — that stuff makes people better at their day jobs whether it happens inside company time or outside it.

The key distinction: I build _in addition to_ managing, not _instead of_ managing. If your 1:1s are getting cancelled so you can tinker with Workers, you have a priority problem, not a side project problem.

## "Isn't This Just Interview Prep?"

Probably the most cynical interpretation — and honestly, I get it. In a market where engineering managers get laid off in waves, maintaining a public portfolio looks an awful lot like keeping your options open.

Here's my honest answer: yes, partially. Having a visible body of work is career insurance. With the reality of layoffs, restructures, and — who knows — maybe AI coming for every knowledge worker (I don't think so, but nobody knows for sure), building your personal identity outside of your role title and current employer matters. I don't think that's shameful. I think it's responsible. The idea that you should be so loyal to your employer that you don't maintain your own professional presence is a deal that only benefits one side.

But the primary motivation is simpler than that. I like building things. I talked about this in [The Side Project as a Management Tool](/blog/the-side-project-as-a-management-tool) — nobody expects their doctor to do surgery on the train for fun, and you definitely don't _have_ to build side projects. But tech is my hobby. I find it genuinely fun. I like learning new tools, solving my own problems, and bettering my skills — both professional and personal — in the process. The career optionality is a side effect, not the goal.

## What I Share and What I Don't

Building in public doesn't mean building without boundaries. The critical thing is being thoughtful about what you're sharing and working on. Am I respecting the IP and contracts with my employer? Is what I'm building far enough away from what they do that it's not competing? Am I making sure nothing proprietary ends up in a blog post?

**I share:** Patterns and approaches from personal projects. Opinions about management and engineering leadership. Technical deep-dives on tools I use in my own time (Terraform, Cloudflare, Astro). Lessons learned from mistakes — mine, not my team's.

**I don't share:** Anything proprietary to my employer. Details about my team's challenges that could identify individuals. Internal architecture or business decisions. Anything that would make a colleague uncomfortable if they stumbled across it.

Your employer doesn't own you. They own your time and output while you're at work. Outside of that, it's your life. As long as you're respecting that boundary, you don't need permission to have a public presence.

## The Thought Leader Thing

I think about people like Travis Neilson from [DevTips](https://www.youtube.com/@DevTipsForDesigners), Wes Bos and Scott Tolinski from [Syntax](https://syntax.fm/), Una Kravets from [Google Chrome's DevRel team](https://una.im/about) — people who are known for their own work and ideas while also being associated with companies. Those companies _benefit_ from having people like that on the team. Thought leaders elevate the organisations they belong to, even when their public work isn't directly about the company.

You don't need to be at that level for the same principle to apply. An engineering manager who writes thoughtfully about their craft is an asset, not a liability. Most companies just haven't figured that out yet. Don't wait for them to.

## The Compounding Effect

The real value of building in public isn't any individual post. It's the compounding. Each article builds on the last. Each side project creates material for the next piece of writing. Each piece of writing solidifies ideas that were half-formed before you tried to explain them.

I learn by doing, and documenting what I've learned forces me to actually understand it. Writing about a concept is the fastest way to find the gaps in your own thinking. The public part is almost secondary — the act of writing is where most of the value lives.

Over time, that body of work becomes yours in a way that a job title never is. It carries across roles, companies, and career pivots.

## The Void

I should be honest about something: right now, I might just be screaming into the void. There's not a huge audience reading these posts. I don't have a massive following. Most of what I publish gets a handful of views and that's it.

That's part of the deal. If you're building in public because you expect immediate traction, you'll quit in a month. If you're doing it because you like building things and writing about them helps you learn — the audience is a bonus, not the point.

## The Practical Reality

If you're a manager thinking about building in public, here's what I'd actually recommend:

**Start with what you already know.** You don't need to learn something new to write about it. The performance review framework you've been using for three years? That's a post. The onboarding process you redesigned? That's a post. The mistake you made in a difficult conversation? That's the _best_ post.

**Write for your past self.** The best technical content answers a question the author had six months ago. You don't need to be an expert — you need to be slightly ahead of your reader and honest about what you don't know.

**Don't ask permission.** I don't mean ignore your company's social media policy. I mean don't wait for your manager to endorse your blog. Don't ask HR if it's okay to have a GitHub profile. The bar is "respect your contracts and don't share proprietary information" — everything else is your life.

## Why Bother

Building in public as a manager is uncomfortable because it makes you visible in a role that traditionally operates behind the scenes. Managers are supposed to be enablers, not protagonists. Putting your name on things feels like it violates that norm.

But the discomfort is worth pushing through. Not because you're going to change the world — but because you'll change yourself. You learn by doing. You solidify ideas by writing them down. And maybe, one person at a time, something you share helps someone else figure out a problem they were stuck on.

That's enough of a reason for me.
