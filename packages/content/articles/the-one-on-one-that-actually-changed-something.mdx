Most 1:1s are status updates wearing a calendar invite. "How's the sprint going?" "Fine." "Any blockers?" "Not really." "Cool, see you next week."

Thirty minutes you'll never get back. Both of you knew it was pointless while it was happening, but neither of you said anything because _that's just what 1:1s are_, right?

Wrong. The best 1:1 I ever had as an IC lasted eleven minutes and changed the trajectory of my career. The best one I've run as a manager was awkward, uncomfortable, and the engineer thanked me for it six months later.

Here's what separates the ones that matter from the ones that don't.

## The Status Update Trap

The default mode for most 1:1s is project status. It feels productive — you're talking about work, after all. But it's a trap. If your 1:1 could be replaced by a Slack message or a standup update, it shouldn't exist.

Status belongs in standups, sprint reviews, and async updates. The 1:1 is the _only_ recurring meeting where a person gets your undivided attention to talk about things that don't fit anywhere else. Don't waste it on things that do.

## What Actually Works

The 1:1s that change something share three characteristics:

### 1. They go where the discomfort is

The most important conversations are the ones neither of you wants to have. "I noticed you've been quieter in planning sessions." "I think you're ready for more than you're getting here." "I don't think the approach you're championing is going to work, and here's why."

These aren't ambush conversations. They're observations delivered with enough trust that the other person doesn't get defensive. That trust doesn't come from the conversation — it comes from every conversation before it.

### 2. They're owned by the report, not the manager

I don't set the agenda for my 1:1s. My direct reports do. My job is to create a space where they can bring the thing they've been chewing on all week — and to notice when they're not bringing it.

The best question I've found: "What's the thing you almost didn't mention?" It's disarming because it acknowledges that people filter. They show up with the safe version. Your job is to make the real version feel safe too.

### 3. They lead to a decision, not just a discussion

A good 1:1 ends with something concrete. Not an action item in the project management sense — a _decision_. "I'm going to put you forward for the tech lead role on Project X." "Let's move your Friday pairing session to Tuesday so you get more overlap with the platform team." "I'll push back on that deadline — you focus on getting the architecture right."

Conversations without decisions are just venting. Venting has its place, but it's not a management tool.

## The Patterns I Keep Coming Back To

**The career 1:1**: Once a quarter, ditch the weekly format entirely. Ask: "Where do you want to be in two years, and what's the gap between here and there?" Then actually build a plan to close the gap. Most managers ask this question and do nothing with the answer. Don't be that manager.

**The silent start**: Sometimes I open a 1:1 with "I've got nothing today — what's on your mind?" and just wait. The silence is uncomfortable, but what fills it is almost always more valuable than whatever I would've said.

**The observation, not the question**: Instead of "How are you going?" try "You seemed frustrated in the retro yesterday." Observations invite honesty. Questions invite performance.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

The reason most 1:1s are bad isn't lack of technique. It's lack of courage. Having a real conversation with someone about their career, their frustrations, their blind spots — that's hard. Status updates are easy.

But nobody ever thanked their manager for asking great standup questions. They thank them for the conversation that helped them see something they couldn't see on their own.

That's the 1:1 worth having.
