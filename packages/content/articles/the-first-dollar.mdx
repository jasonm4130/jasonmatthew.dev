Building FormRecap took a weekend. Getting anyone to notice it has taken significantly longer — and I'm still working on it.

## There Was No Fairytale Launch

I'd read the Hacker News success stories. "I built X over a weekend and woke up to 10,000 users." I half-expected something like that. I shipped it, wrote a Dev.to deep-dive on the architecture, posted on Twitter, and... crickets. Not silence exactly — a few likes, a few comments — but nothing close to the tidal wave you imagine when you're heads-down building.

Then I tried the obvious channels. Reddit penalises self-promotion heavily — multiple subreddits either removed my posts or shadowbanned them. Fair enough, those communities exist for discussion, not product launches. But even the "Show HN"-style subreddits were hostile to new accounts posting links. Most platforms are built this way now, and honestly, maybe they should be. The channels that actually work require authentic long-term engagement, not drive-by product drops.

## The LinkedIn Revelation

The LinkedIn experience was the one that really stuck with me. My launch post about FormRecap got three comment threads. Not one of them asked what I'd built. Not one showed any curiosity about the product, the architecture, the problem it solves. Every single comment was someone pushing their own engagement — "Great post! Have you seen my tool that does X?" or the classic "Love this! 🔥 DM me, I help founders scale."

It's a platform optimised for performance, not conversation. Everyone is broadcasting. Nobody is listening.

That might sound bitter, but it was actually clarifying. It taught me that marketing a side project is a completely different skill from building one, and the platforms I assumed would help are designed for something else entirely.

## Why I'm Persisting

I'm going to keep at it until I get that first paying customer. Not because I expect FormRecap to replace my salary — it won't, and that's fine. I'm doing this because I think every engineer, especially every engineering manager, should understand what it takes to get someone to pay you money for something you built.

Not through a salary. Not through a contract your company won. Directly: here's a product, here's the price, do you want it?

That transaction teaches you things about product thinking, positioning, and user empathy that no management course ever will. You learn very quickly that "I built something cool" is not a value proposition. You learn that the features you're proud of are not the features people care about. You learn that "if you build it, they will come" is the most dangerous lie in software.

## The Freelancing Callback

I freelanced early in my career — WordPress sites, small business branding, the whole thing. I remember what that first invoice felt like. Someone looked at work I did and decided it was worth paying for. It wasn't a lot of money, but it was transformative. It changed how I thought about the value of work.

I want to feel that again, in a different context. Not building websites for local businesses, but shipping a SaaS product to strangers on the internet who find it, try it, and decide it's worth $29 a month. That's a different kind of validation, and I haven't earned it yet.

## What I'd Tell Other Engineers

If you're thinking about launching something: build it, absolutely. But budget as much time for marketing as you did for building — maybe more. The engineering is the easy part. Finding the people who need what you made and convincing them to trust you with their money? That's the actual hard problem.

And if your LinkedIn launch post gets nothing but engagement farmers in the comments, know that it's not just you. That's the platform working as designed.
