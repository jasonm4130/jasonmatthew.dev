Every performance review cycle, the same ritual plays out. Managers stare at a blank text box for twenty minutes, write something that could apply to literally anyone on the team, and hit submit. Engineers read it, feel vaguely unsatisfied, and go back to work.

We've all been on both sides of this. The review that says "consistently delivers high-quality work and is a valued team member" tells you absolutely nothing. It's a participation trophy in paragraph form.

I've written a lot of bad reviews. I've also received a few genuinely great ones — the kind where you read them and think "okay, this person actually _sees_ me." Here's what I've learned about the difference.

## The Test: Would You Pay for This Feedback?

Before you submit a review, ask yourself: if this person had hired an executive coach for $300/hour, would the coach's feedback look anything like what you've written?

If the answer is no — if your review is vaguer, softer, and less actionable than what a stranger with a clipboard could produce — you haven't done your job.

A performance review is the most concentrated feedback most engineers get all year. Treat it like the scarce resource it is.

## What Bad Reviews Have in Common

**They describe _what_ without explaining _why it matters_.** "Delivered the authentication rewrite on time" is a fact, not feedback. "Delivered the authentication rewrite on time, and the way you broke the migration into reversible steps meant we had zero production incidents — that's the kind of risk management that separates senior engineers from mid-levels" is feedback.

**They avoid anything uncomfortable.** If someone's review is 100% positive, either they're perfect (unlikely) or you're conflict-averse (likely). The kindest thing you can do is name the gap. Not to punish — to give them something to work on.

**They use company jargon as a crutch.** "Demonstrates our value of customer obsession" is meaningless. "Spent two days investigating a performance issue that only affected 3% of users because she understood that those users had disproportionate contract value" is specific, memorable, and actually reinforces the behaviour you want to see more of.

## A Framework That Works

I structure every review around three things:

### 1. The Pattern

Not individual events — the _pattern_ across the review period. One great pull request is a data point. Consistently writing pull requests that other engineers use as reference material is a pattern. Patterns are more useful than highlights because they describe _who someone is becoming_, not just what they did.

### 2. The Impact

Every piece of feedback should connect to impact. Not "you mentored two junior engineers" but "the two engineers you mentored both shipped independently this quarter for the first time — that's a direct result of how you structured your pairing sessions."

This is harder to write because it requires you to actually _notice_ impact, which means paying attention during the quarter instead of cramming at review time. There's no shortcut for this. If you're writing reviews from memory the week they're due, you've already lost.

### 3. The Edge

What's the next level look like, and what's the specific gap between where they are and where they could be? Not "continue to grow your leadership skills" — that's astrology. Try "you're strong at technical decision-making within your team, but I haven't seen you influence decisions across teams yet. The API standardisation initiative next quarter would be a good place to practice that."

## The Uncomfortable Ones

The hardest reviews to write are for people who are underperforming. The temptation is to soften everything until the message evaporates. "There are some areas for growth" means "you're in trouble" but sounds like "you're doing fine."

Don't do this. An engineer who's struggling deserves to _know_ they're struggling, clearly and without ambiguity, so they can make an informed decision about what to do next. Burying the message in euphemisms isn't kind — it's cowardly. It delays the conversation they need to have and robs them of the chance to course-correct.

That doesn't mean being harsh. It means being honest. "Your output has dropped significantly this quarter. I don't know if that's burnout, boredom, or something else — but I want to figure it out with you, because the current trajectory isn't sustainable" is direct without being cruel.

## The Process

Here's what I actually do:

1. **Keep a running doc per person throughout the quarter.** Every time someone does something noteworthy — good or bad — I write 1-2 sentences. This takes 30 seconds and saves hours of "what did they do again?" at review time.
2. **Write the growth area first.** It's the hardest part, and if you leave it for last, you'll either skip it or phone it in.
3. **Read it from their perspective.** Would you find this useful if you received it? Would you know _exactly_ what to do more of, less of, and differently? If not, rewrite.
4. **Say it in person first.** Nothing in a written review should be a surprise. If it is, the review isn't the problem — your feedback cadence is.

## The Standard

Write the review you wish your manager had written for you. Specific enough that it could only describe one person. Honest enough that it might be uncomfortable. Actionable enough that they can do something with it on Monday.

That's the bar. Most reviews don't clear it. Yours should.
