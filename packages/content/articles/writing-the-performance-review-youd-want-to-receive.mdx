Performance reviews make everyone nervous. Managers stare at the blank text box, unsure how to compress six months of work into a few paragraphs. Engineers refresh the page wondering whether they're about to read something that changes their day. Both sides dread it, and that shared anxiety is usually what makes the whole thing bad.

It doesn't have to be this way.

I have one rule that keeps me honest through every review cycle: **nothing in a performance review should be said for the first time.** If I'm writing something the person hasn't already heard from me in a 1:1, I've failed — not at the review, but at the months of management that preceded it.

A performance review should be a summary of conversations you've already had. Confirming things you've discussed, following up on actions you've agreed on, and documenting where you're headed together. If it feels like a surprise, the review isn't the problem — your feedback cadence is.

## What Bad Reviews Have in Common

**They describe _what_ without explaining _why it matters_.** "Delivered the authentication rewrite on time" is a fact, not feedback. "Delivered the authentication rewrite on time, and the way you broke the migration into reversible steps meant we had zero production incidents" is feedback. The difference is impact.

**They avoid anything uncomfortable.** If someone's review is 100% positive, either they're perfect (unlikely) or you've been avoiding a conversation you should have had months ago (likely). The kindest thing you can do is name the gap — not to punish, but to give them something to work on.

**They use company jargon as a crutch.** "Demonstrates our value of customer obsession" is meaningless. "Spent two days investigating a performance issue that only affected 3% of users because she understood that those users had disproportionate contract value" is specific, memorable, and actually reinforces the behaviour you want to see more of.

## A Simple Framework

The formal name for what I do is [SBI — Situation, Behavior, Impact](https://www.ccl.org/articles/leading-effectively-articles/closing-the-gap-between-intent-vs-impact-sbii/). But in plain language, it's three things: **what you did, why it mattered, and what's next.**

### What you did

Not individual events — the patterns across the review period. I keep a running doc for each person throughout the cycle. Every time someone does something noteworthy — good or bad — I write a sentence or two. "You did X and it had Y impact." "In this situation, this approach didn't land well — here's what I observed." It takes thirty seconds in the moment and saves hours of "what did they do again?" at review time.

One great pull request is a data point. Consistently writing pull requests that other engineers use as reference material is a pattern. Patterns describe _who someone is becoming_, not just what they did on a given Tuesday.

### Why it mattered

Every piece of feedback should connect to impact. Not "you mentored two junior engineers" but "the two engineers you mentored both shipped independently this quarter for the first time — that's a direct result of how you structured your pairing sessions."

This is harder to write because it requires you to actually _notice_ impact. If you're writing reviews from memory the week they're due, you've already lost. The running doc is how you avoid that trap.

### What's next

This is where the review becomes useful beyond a backward-looking report card. I try to frame reviews around outcomes — what do we actually want from this process? What should this person be working towards? Once we've got that, the "what's working" and "what needs to change" become the path to get there, not abstract judgements.

Not "continue to grow your leadership skills" — that's astrology. Try "you're strong at technical decision-making within your team, but I haven't seen you influence decisions across teams yet. The API standardisation initiative next quarter would be a good place to practice that."

## The Hard Reviews

The hardest reviews to write are for people who are underperforming. The temptation is to soften everything until the message evaporates. "There are some areas for growth" means "you're in trouble" but sounds like "you're doing fine."

Here's the thing though — if you've been following the "nothing new" rule, this review shouldn't be a shock. You've already had the conversations. You've already named the gap in your 1:1s. The review is documenting a trajectory you've both been watching, not dropping a bomb.

That doesn't make it easy. Being direct with someone about underperformance is hard even when they're expecting it. I won't pretend I get it right every time. But approaching it head-on, with the person's best interest genuinely at heart, consistently leads to better outcomes than softening the message until it means nothing.

One thing I always do before a hard review conversation: check in first. Yes, they showed up to the meeting. But maybe they've had a terrible morning. Maybe life has happened and they can't cope with a heavy conversation right now. Give them the option. "We're going to talk about some difficult stuff today — are you in the right headspace for that, or should we reschedule?" It costs you nothing and it shows the person you see them as a human, not a line item.

## Good Performers Come First

There's a tendency to think of performance management as something you do _to_ underperformers. I think that's backwards. Performance starts with keeping your good performers performing well. Recognising the things they're doing right, making sure they know their work is seen, giving them growth opportunities before they have to ask.

If you only pay attention to performance when someone's struggling, you'll lose the people who are excelling long before you notice. A review that says "consistently delivers high-quality work and is a valued team member" might technically be positive, but it tells the person nothing. It's a participation trophy in paragraph form. Your best people deserve better than that.

## The Process

Here's what I actually do:

1. **Keep the running doc updated throughout the cycle.** Specific examples with impact — both strengths and improvement areas. This is the single most important thing. Everything else falls apart without it.
2. **Start with outcomes.** What do we want this person to be working towards? Frame the review around that destination, then use what's working and what needs to change as the map to get there.
3. **Write the growth area first.** It's the hardest part, and if you leave it for last, you'll either skip it or phone it in.
4. **Read it from their perspective.** Would you find this useful if you received it? Would you know _exactly_ what to do more of, less of, and differently? If not, rewrite.

## The Point

Performance reviews don't have to be something either side dreads. If you've been having honest conversations throughout the year — the kind we talked about in [the 1:1 article](/blog/the-one-on-one-that-actually-changed-something) — the review should feel like a natural checkpoint, not a judgement day.

A good review shouldn't surprise anyone. It should feel like a summary of conversations you've already had — and a plan for where you're going next.
